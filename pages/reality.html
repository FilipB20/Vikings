Introduction

The TV series *Vikings* has captured the imagination of viewers around the world with its action-packed storytelling and dramatic portrayal of Viking life. However, many elements of the show are dramatized for entertainment purposes. In this section, we will explore the differences and similarities between the events and characters in the series and their real-life counterparts. From historical facts to cultural practices, let's uncover what the real Vikings were like compared to their depiction in the show.

Ragnar Lodbrok

Ragnar in the TV Series

In the *Vikings* TV series, Ragnar Lodbrok is portrayed as a charismatic and ambitious farmer who becomes a legendary Viking king. His thirst for adventure and exploration drives him to raid England, leading his people into battle, including his famed journey to Paris. His personality in the show blends myth with reality, as he is depicted as both a loving father and a ruthless conqueror.

The Real Ragnar Lodbrok

Historically, Ragnar Lodbrok may not have existed as a single individual, but rather as a composite of various Viking heroes and kings. Some scholars believe he could have been based on real figures like Ragnar Sigurdsson, a Viking king, and his legendary sons, who became famous warriors. Ragnar's story is steeped in Norse sagas, but the details of his life are speculative at best.

Lagertha

Lagertha in the TV Series

Lagertha in the *Vikings* series is depicted as a fierce shieldmaiden and warrior. She fights alongside her husband, Ragnar, and even leads troops into battle. Lagertha’s character in the series is empowered, strong, and an icon of female warriors.

The Real Lagertha

While Lagertha may have been based on a historical figure, the real Lagertha remains more of a mythic character. In historical texts, Lagertha is mentioned as a shieldmaiden in Saxo Grammaticus' *Gesta Danorum* (The Deeds of the Danes). Though there is little to prove she existed in the same capacity as portrayed in the show, many scholars believe that shieldmaidens did, in fact, exist in Norse society, even if their roles were not as dominant as the show suggests.

Bjorn Ironside

Bjorn in the TV Series

Bjorn Ironside, Ragnar’s son, is portrayed as a strong, honorable, and capable warrior in the TV series. Throughout the show, he proves his worth in battle and leadership, becoming a key figure in Viking history.

The Real Bjorn Ironside

The real Bjorn Ironside was indeed Ragnar’s son, but much of his portrayal in the series is exaggerated. He is often cited in sagas and medieval texts as a renowned warrior and leader, who explored parts of the Mediterranean and raided areas such as Spain and Italy. His achievements, however, may have been romanticized over time.

Viking Culture: Series vs Reality

The Viking Way of Life in the TV Series

The *Vikings* TV series presents a rough and violent Viking society that engages in constant raiding, pillaging, and warfare. The characters frequently display their love for battle, honor, and their gods, particularly Odin and Thor. Norse paganism is prominently featured, as is the warrior ethos.

The Real Viking Culture

While the Vikings were indeed skilled warriors and raiders, their society was also rich in trade, farming, craftsmanship, and exploration. The real Vikings were advanced shipbuilders, navigators, and traders who established settlements far beyond Scandinavia. Their raids were part of a larger network of trade and colonization, with many Vikings settling in foreign lands such as the British Isles, Iceland, Greenland, and even parts of North America. Norse mythology was deeply intertwined with their daily life, but their society was more diverse than simply a warrior culture.

The Viking Raids: Series vs Reality

Viking Raids in the TV Series

The Viking raids in *Vikings* are depicted as brutal and relentless. The most famous raid is the attack on the Anglo-Saxon monastery at Lindisfarne in 793 AD, which is shown as a pivotal moment in the series. These raids are often motivated by the desire for riches, glory, and divine favor.

The Real Viking Raids

The real Viking raids did indeed begin around the late 8th century, with the attack on Lindisfarne being a landmark event. However, the Vikings' motivations were more complex. They raided not just for treasure, but to assert power, control trade routes, and gain strategic footholds in foreign lands. The Vikings were also well-organized, often using surprise attacks and quick escapes to their advantage.
